# Venture-Grade Market Validation: "Jigged" — A No-Upside Sports-Betting Simulator

## TL;DR
- **VERDICT: INVALIDATED as specced (high confidence, ~80%).** There is no demonstrated willingness to pay a recurring subscription for a pure, no-upside sports-betting simulator. The single closest direct comparable — WagerLab, which already runs the exact $4.99/month model on fake-money sports parlays — reached only the 100,000+ Android install tier over ~6–7 years on pre-seed funding, is sub-scale on iOS, and its paywall draws active user hostility. Every adjacent product that DID scale (Fliff, Betr, Thrillzz) monetizes on the *aspiration of real money/prizes*, which Jigged explicitly removes.
- **The two-door positioning is internally contradictory and likely unsafe for Door A.** Peer-reviewed clinical literature (Clark et al. and multiple replications) shows that simulated wagering and "near-miss" loss outcomes recruit the brain's win-related reward circuitry and *increase* motivation to gamble. A product that asks a recovering gambler to keep building parlays is the opposite of what cessation tools (Gamban, BetBlocker) do — block and abstain — and no harm-reduction org would distribute it. Door B (risk-free entertainment) cannibalizes itself: the entertainment IS the money upside, and free sweepstakes books already own that niche.
- **Acquisition is the harder of the two kill-risks.** Paid channels (Apple/Google/Meta) restrict gambling-adjacent and "simulated gambling" creative; the influencer channel is structurally conflicted because sports creators earn $100–300 CPA promoting real sportsbooks; and the clinical-referral channel will not refer patients to a simulator. The "Quittr 25% conversion" analogy does not transfer: Quittr sells *abstinence*; Jigged sells *continued simulation of the addictive behavior*.

## Key Findings

**1. The exact business model already exists and has failed to scale.** WagerLab, Inc. ships a free fake-money sports-betting app monetized by a $4.99/month "VIP" subscription — identical price, identical no-real-money mechanic (it tracks only valueless "units"), identical "build parlays risk-free" pitch. After roughly six to seven years it sits in Google Play's "100,000+" install bracket (~1,950 ratings, 3.9 stars), has too few iOS ratings for Apple to even display a score, and has raised only a pre-seed round (investors Stadia Ventures and Splash Sports). Its store reviews repeatedly attack the paywall ("basically everything is hidden behind the VIP subscription… you may as well spend it on actual bets"). This is the single most important datapoint in the analysis: the model has been run, and the market verdict is "small." No other pure no-upside sports-betting sim found has reached meaningful scale (e.g., "FakeBet" sits at ~850 lifetime downloads).

**2. Everything that scaled in this category monetizes on real-money upside — the exact thing Jigged removes.** Fliff (reported 3–5M registered users; ~$25.6M revenue; $15M raised, Series B 2022) is explicitly retained by *redeemable* Fliff Cash; its own app reviews are dominated by "you can win real money" and "it's free." Fliff makes roughly 85% of revenue from coin-package IAP, not subscriptions. Betr raised $50M (Jake Paul/Joey Levy; later reported $375M valuation) but has retreated — exiting its Virginia sportsbook in July 2025, keeping its real-money sportsbook live only in Ohio, and pivoting toward DFS/prediction markets/social casino. Social casinos convert only about 12% of users to payers versus more than 50% on licensed gambling apps (per RG.org's 2024–25 sweepstakes-casino analysis), and they depend on whales — the top 2–5% of players can generate up to 80% of in-app revenue — chasing variable-reward outcomes. That monetization engine is driven by the *hope of winning*, is structurally unavailable to a no-upside product, and is not a flat subscription.

**3. The "near-miss" mechanic is a clinical liability for the recovery door.** The savings-ledger ("money you didn't lose") is functionally a near-miss reinforcement loop. Clark et al. (PMC2658737) found near-misses "recruit striatal and insula circuitry that also responded to monetary wins" and "invigorate gambling through the anomalous recruitment of reward circuitry, despite the objective lack of monetary reinforcement." Multiple studies show that a ~30% near-miss frequency *increases* gambling persistence. A product that keeps a problem gambler placing simulated parlays — and dramatizes how close they came — risks reinforcing the urge it claims to treat. Cessation tools do the opposite: Gamban (£24.99/yr) and BetBlocker (free, charity-run, 17k+ daily iOS users) *block* gambling and deliberately put friction between user and bet.

**4. Willingness-to-pay for no-stakes versions of real-money activities is structurally weak.** The cleanest analog — paper-trading / stock simulators (Investopedia, thinkorswim paperMoney, Webull) — are universally free and used as funnels into real trading; essentially nobody pays a standalone subscription for the simulation itself. Where recurring subscriptions DO work in personal finance (Qapital, ~$3–12/mo, ~2M users), the product delivers a *real* outcome (actual saved dollars), not a simulated one.

**5. Acquisition economics are hostile.** Real-money gambling ads are restricted on Apple, Google, and Meta. Per Google's 2025 Ads Safety Report (published April 16, 2026), gambling/gaming was the eighth-largest banned-ad category at 270.7 million ads removed and the third-largest restricted category at 123.9 million. Meta will allow social-casino/F2P ads only where there is "no opportunity to win money, coins, gifts or anything of monetary value" — which a pure sim might technically qualify for, but "simulated gambling" age-gating and manual review friction remain. The influencer channel is structurally conflicted: sports creators monetize via sportsbook affiliate deals ($100–300 CPA for sportsbook, plus 30–50% RevShare from DraftKings/FanDuel), so promoting a *loss-reduction* app directly cannibalizes their primary income. The clinical/harm-reduction referral channel — the only "cheap" path for Door A — will not refer patients to a gamble-simulator.

**6. The regulatory tailwind the concept implicitly leans on is closing.** California's AB 831 (effective Jan 1, 2026) bans dual-currency sweepstakes sportsbooks (explicitly including Fliff), and at least ~16 states are moving similarly. That removes a competitor but also signals that "social sportsbook" framing draws regulatory and tribal hostility — and the states clearing out are precisely the "non-betting states" Door B targets, where free CFTC-regulated prediction markets (Kalshi, Polymarket, and now DraftKings/Robinhood entrants) are rushing in.

### Willingness-to-Pay read
- Subscription benchmarks (RevenueCat State of Subscription Apps): hard paywalls convert downloads to paid at a median of ~10.7% vs freemium's 2.1%, and median 14-day revenue is roughly 8x higher for hard-paywall apps. But only 17.3% of newly launched apps reach even $1K MRR within two years, and per RevenueCat's 2025 report "across all categories, 82% of subscription app trial starts still happen on day zero." That means Jigged must sell a *promise* in the first session, before any "savings ledger" can accrue value. The ledger payoff is inherently slow-building, fighting the Day-0 conversion reality.
- The Quittr comparison the founder leans on is a *false analog*. Per RevenueCat, founder Alex Slater "scaled it to over $3m ARR within its first year," on a hard paywall at "$12.99 monthly or $45 yearly," with onboarding cited as a ~25% download-to-paid converter. But Quittr sells abstinence from a behavior the user already wants to stop, with a guilt/identity hook ("invest in yourself," sign your name). Jigged asks the user to keep performing the addictive behavior in simulated form — a fundamentally weaker and ethically muddier value proposition for the recovery segment.

### TAM read
- **Door A (problem/help-seeking sports gamblers):** 22% of US adults bet money on sports in the past year (Pew Research, survey of 9,916 adults conducted July 8–Aug. 3, 2025; up from 19% three years prior). Among sports bettors, ~6–10% meet problem-gambling criteria, with NCPG citing higher disordered-gambling rates among online sports bettors specifically. Critically, per the Siena College/St. Bonaventure "American Sports Fanship Survey" (released Feb. 18, 2025), "only 9% of bettors have ever called a problem gambling helpline or sought other help to control gambling behaviors." So the help-seeking pool is a fraction of a fraction — and the safest sub-segment (active recovery) is exactly the group the near-miss mechanic may harm.
- **Door B (risk-free-sim sports fans / non-betting states):** Larger raw pool, but low-intent and already served free by Fliff-style sweepstakes books (with prize upside) and, increasingly, by CFTC-regulated prediction markets entering CA/TX. Asking this casual, entertainment-seeking user to pay $4.99/mo for *less* than the free alternatives offer is the core demand problem.

### The two doors cannibalize
A recovery user wants to stop; an entertainment user wants the thrill (which comes from stakes/upside). A single app optimized for "keep building parlays" serves the entertainment frame while undermining the recovery frame, and the recovery framing ("money you didn't lose") deflates the entertainment thrill. The AI persona "The Sharp" (cold-math intervention) further dampens entertainment. The positioning is a straddle that satisfies neither segment fully.

### The convex alternative (brief)
A "Qapital/Digit for gamblers" — link real bank/sportsbook accounts, detect real gambling losses, and auto-route *matched real savings* into a HYSA/brokerage — is materially more defensible because it produces a *real* financial outcome (real saved/invested dollars), which is the one thing people reliably pay subscriptions for in fintech (Qapital ~$3–12/mo, ~2M users). Precedent exists in pieces: Qapital's "spend less" and IFTTT round-up rules, Acorns round-ups, and gambling-transaction blocking by banks/Gamban. No player currently owns the avoided-loss→savings mechanic specifically for gamblers at scale. This is the more investable direction, though it carries open-banking, data-permission, and "are you encouraging continued gambling to generate savings?" design risks.

### Top 3 Kill-Risks (ranked)
1. **No willingness-to-pay for no-upside simulation.** Proven by WagerLab's flat ceiling on the identical model and by the universal free-ness of paper-trading sims. This is the primary, near-fatal risk.
2. **User acquisition is structurally blocked.** Restricted paid-ad creative, conflicted influencers (sportsbook CPA cannibalization), and a clinical channel that won't refer to a simulator. Even if demand existed, CAC likely exceeds a $4.99/mo LTV.
3. **Door A is clinically counter-indicated and reputationally radioactive.** Near-miss reinforcement literature means the recovery use case may worsen outcomes, foreclosing harm-reduction distribution and inviting criticism.

### Top 2 Conditions That Would Have to Be True
1. **A no-upside sim must demonstrate durable, payable engagement that WagerLab never achieved** — e.g., a hard-paywall cohort showing ≥10% Day-0 trial-start and ≥8% D30 retention (above social-casino's 2.3–5.4%).
2. **At least one compliant, non-conflicted acquisition channel must exist at a CAC below sub-$60 (≈12 months of subscription gross).** Without a viable channel, the product cannot be acquired profitably regardless of demand.

## Recommendations
1. **Do not build Jigged as specced (pure no-upside sim, dual-door, subscription).** The closest comp has already proven the ceiling is low. Confidence is high enough to stop here.
2. **If you proceed at all, kill Door A entirely.** The clinical near-miss evidence makes the recovery positioning both ineffective and a reputational/liability risk; harm-reduction distribution (the cheap channel) will refuse it. Threshold to revisit: a published RCT or clinician endorsement showing simulated betting reduces (not reinforces) urges — which current literature contradicts.
3. **Pressure-test demand before any build with a fake-door/paywall test.** Run a hard-paywall landing test ($4.99/mo) against cold paid traffic on the one channel likely to clear policy (Meta "no monetary value" social-gaming creative) and measure Day-0 trial-start and 14-day retention. **Go/no-go threshold:** trial-start ≥10% and projected D30 retention ≥8%. Below that, abandon.
4. **Strongly prefer pivoting to the convex savings-fintech model.** It converts the same insight ("the money you didn't lose is real money") into an actual, payable financial outcome, sidesteps the simulation/near-miss trap, and aligns with proven fintech-subscription WTP. Validate with a waitlist + bank-link intent test before committing.
5. **Whatever you build, solve acquisition first, not product.** Secure at least one viable, policy-compliant paid channel and one non-conflicted distribution partner (e.g., an employer-EAP or health-plan harm-reduction contract for the fintech version) before writing app code.

## Caveats
- Several Fliff figures (revenue ~$25.6M, 3–5M users, sub-$50 CAC, retention uplifts) come from secondary aggregators and Fliff-adjacent "business model" content sites of mixed reliability; treat them as estimates, not audited. The $15M funding and the AB 831 ban are well-corroborated.
- Exact download numbers for WagerLab and other pure sims are paywalled (Sensor Tower/Apptopia/SimilarWeb); the "100K+" Android bracket and rating counts are hard data from store metadata, but precise installs and DAU/MAU are not public. (One Sensor Tower snapshot showed a single recent month at ~70K downloads / ~$30K revenue for Fliff's app, illustrating month-to-month volatility — not a stable figure.)
- App Store "simulated gambling" rejection risk was treated as assumed-solvable per instructions; note that the same classification also constrains paid-ad creative, which feeds directly into the (un-assumed-away) acquisition problem.
- Problem-gambling prevalence figures vary by source/methodology (6–10% of bettors vs NCPG's higher disordered-gambling estimates among online bettors); ranges are reported rather than a single point.
- The convex-alternative assessment is deliberately brief and directional, not a full validation; it carries its own unexamined regulatory (open-banking, money-movement) and ethical risks.
